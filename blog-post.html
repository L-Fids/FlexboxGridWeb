Basic Sourdough Recipe

  • Logan Fidler
  • May 30, 2020

This recipe is the workhorse in your bread stable. If you learn to make this properly and consistently, branching off into other varieties of bread will be easy and natural.

Flour, water, salt and a levain (AKA a leavening agent) are all you need to get this recipe going. The following proportion of ingredients will render you two smaller sized loaves. Perfect for an individual or small family.

Ingredients

White Flour: 604 grams

Whole Wheat Flour: 276 grams

Water: 684 grams

Fine Sea Salt: 22 grams

Starter: 216 grams

Instructions

  1. Feed your starter: 24 hours after your previous feeding, discard all but 50 grams. Add 100 grams all purpose flour and 50 grams whole wheat. Mix until incorporated and let rest for 6-8 hours.
  2. Autolyse: After your starter rests, mix the 604 grams white and 276 grams whole wheat flour with 684 grams water in a stand mixer just until incorporated. Be careful not to overmix at this point. Cover and let rest for 20-30 minutes.
  3. Mix final dough: In the stand mixer bowl, sprinkle 22 grams salt over the top of the dough. Measure out 216 grams of starter into a separate bowl. Transfer measured out starter into stand mixer bowl with a spatula. Mix for 90 seconds at low speed. Transfer to large bowl for bulk fermentation.
  4. Folding and bulk fermentation: This dough requires 4 - 6 folds during bulk fermentation. Aim to perform the first fold about 20 minutes after mixing with a 20 minute gap between the next two folds. Folds 4 - 6 can be done 30 minutes apart. After the final fold, let dough rest until at least doubled in size, approx 4 - 6 hours depending on room temperature. If room temp is too cool (less than 20C), dough can be placed in oven with oven light on to increase fermentation temperature.
  5. Divide: Sprinkle flour around the edges of the dough. With a spatula, gently ease out the dough onto a floured work surface, ensuring the dough does not tear. With a dough cutter, cut the dough in half. It helps here to have floured hands and access to a shallow bowl of water into which you can dip the dough cutter as the dough will be sticky.
  6. Shape: With a floured non-dominant hand and dough cutter in your dominant hand, shape each dough into a ball. Let rest for 5 minutes. Though this next step is not necessary I have found it helps with gas retention. Do a final shape by flipping the dough and folding it in half and repeat the process of forming the dough into a medium-tight ball. Let rest for 5 minutes again. Place the shaped doughs, seam side up, into pre-floured proofing bowls.
  7. Proof: Place proofing bowls in individual plastic bags and then place in fridge to let proof for 12 hours.
  8. Bake Prep: Place dutch oven(s) in the oven and preheat to 500F. Once the oven has reached 500F, let the dutch ovens continue to heat in the oven for at least another 30 minutes. You want those dutch ovens nice and hot.
  9. Bake: Pull your doughs out of the fridge. Cut two parchment papers the width of the dutch ovens. Gently overturn your doughs onto the parchment paper. With a razor, score the top of your bread with two clean slices approx 2 cm in depth. This will help ensure the gases built up in your dough escape upwards and loft your bread upward rather than sideways. Take out the dutch ovens and from the oven and carefully lower the doughs into the dutch ovens with mitted hands. Mist the tops of the doughs with water. Bake for 15 mins at 500F. Lower temp to 450F and bake for 15 more mins. Remove lid and bake for another 15-20 minutes until bread is golden brown.
  10. Cool: Your bread is baked and now all you have to do is let it cool for 1-2 hours on a cooling rack.
